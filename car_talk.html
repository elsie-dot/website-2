Tyre Types and what they do:

Tyre Type Colour When are they used
Soft Red For maximum grip and the fastest lap times, commonly for qualifying sessions and short, fast race stints
Medium Yellow Used in dry conditions, they are slower than soft tyres but last longer
Hard White Used in dry conditions, they are the slowest and take longer to warm up but last the longest
Intermediate Green Used in light rain, they have grooves to disperse water and prevent aquaplaning
Wet Blue Used in heavy rain, they have deeper grooves to disperse more water and provide better grip

What does the back wing accomplish?

The rear wing of a Formula 1 car is designed to generate downforce, which helps to increase the car's grip on the track. The rear wing works by creating a pressure difference between the upper and lower surfaces of the wing. As air flows over the wing, it is forced to travel faster over the upper surface than the lower surface, creating a low-pressure area above the wing and a high-pressure area below it. This pressure difference generates downforce, which pushes the car down onto the track and allows it to corner at higher speeds without losing traction.

In addition to generating downforce, the rear wing also helps to reduce drag, which is the resistance that the car experiences as it moves through the air. The rear wing is designed to be as aerodynamic as possible, with a shape that minimizes drag while still providing enough downforce to keep the car stable at high speeds.

The drivers are able to activate a DRS Mode which open up the wing which boots the cars on straights
